Healthcare Data Interoperability & Remote Patient Monitoring

The Interoperability Market: Regulatory Tailwind at Full Force

The healthcare interoperability solutions market reached $5.04 billion in 2025 and is projected to grow to $8.62 billion by 2030 at an 11.31% CAGR, with some estimates projecting the broader healthcare data interoperability market reaching $14 billion by 2032 at 13.4% CAGR. In the U.S. specifically, the interoperability market is expected to grow from $1.82 billion in 2025 to $6.29 billion by 2035 at a 13.2% CAGR. The regulatory environment is the primary accelerant, with multiple federal mandates creating compliance urgency that translates directly into infrastructure spending.

Sources: Mordor Intelligence (2025), PS Market Research (2025), SNS Insider (2026)

TEFCA (Trusted Exchange Framework and Common Agreement) went live in 2024 and is rapidly becoming the national standard for health data exchange. Epic's subsidiary Epic Nexus was among the first organizations to achieve Qualified Health Information Network (QHIN) designation, connecting over 625 hospitals through TEFCA in 2024 alone. Oracle Health achieved QHIN status in November 2025. CMS requires FHIR-based APIs for prior authorization and patient access by 2026. HHS finalized HTI-2 and HTI-3 rules establishing TEFCA governance and reproductive-health data protections in January 2025. The framework is no longer aspirational—it is operational and expanding rapidly.

Sources: Epic (2024), ONC/ASTP (2025), Ropes & Gray analysis (Jan 2025)

The adoption metrics confirm the acceleration. According to the ASTP/ONC, standardized FHIR-based API adoption in U.S. inpatient settings rose to 69%. Eighty-one percent of U.S. hospitals now allow third-party application access to health records, enabling modular, multi-vendor ecosystem connectivity. Microsoft Azure's FHIR service surpassed 1 billion monthly API calls in 2024, demonstrating elastic cloud-scale performance. Information-blocking penalties of up to $1 million per violation under the 21st Century Cures Act have made API standardization a board-level imperative for every hospital CIO in the country.

Sources: ASTP/ONC (2025), Microsoft (2024), Mordor Intelligence (2025)

Remote Patient Monitoring: Clinical Billing at Scale

The global remote patient monitoring market was valued at $27.7 billion in 2024 and is projected to reach $56.9 billion by 2030 at a 12.7% CAGR. The growth drivers are structural: rising chronic disease prevalence, an aging population, the shift to value-based care, and the post-pandemic normalization of virtual care delivery. By 2030, IQVIA projects that over 142 million U.S. patients—nearly 40% of the population—will be using RPM technologies.

Sources: MarketsandMarkets (2025), IQVIA

The reimbursement structure makes RPM a compelling revenue stream. CMS CPT codes allow providers to bill approximately $110–150 per patient per month for combined RPM (CPT 99453/54/57/58) and chronic care management (CPT 99490) services. CMS introduced new CPT codes in 2025 further expanding the financial viability of RPM, especially for chronic care programs. A VA health services study found that RPM for chronic diseases reduced hospital admissions by up to 25% and achieved cost savings of $2,000 per patient per year. Eighty-five percent of physicians recognize RPM as beneficial for chronic disease management.

Sources: CMS (2025), U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs, AMA (2023)

The intersection of RPM and value-based care creates compound revenue. A provider practice connected to infrastructure can bill ACCESS OAPs for enrolled Medicare beneficiaries AND bill RPM/CCM codes for non-ACCESS patients (commercial, Medicare Advantage, Medicaid) at the same practice under the same infrastructure. These are not competing revenue streams—they are additive. The ACCESS patient generates OAP revenue. The commercially insured patient at the same practice generates RPM/CCM revenue. The same data infrastructure, the same devices, the same clinical workflows serve both populations.

The Compound Revenue Opportunity

Interoperability + RPM + VBC = Infrastructure Compounding

The interoperability and RPM markets are not standalone opportunities for TETRA—they are the enabling infrastructure layers that make the VBC and D2C markets accessible. A provider practice connected through TETRA eX (interoperability) can simultaneously participate in ACCESS (VBC), bill RPM/CCM codes for non-ACCESS patients (RPM), refer patients to the consumer app (D2C), and have all AI interactions governed (AI Infrastructure). The four markets are not additive—they are multiplicative. Each infrastructure layer enables revenue from the others.

The TEFCA regulatory tailwind is particularly significant because it creates urgency. Information-blocking penalties of up to $1 million per violation mean that every hospital CIO, every health system CEO, and every practice administrator is now asking the same question: are we compliant? The compliance urgency creates the opening. The infrastructure relationship created for compliance becomes the foundation for VBC participation, RPM billing, consumer referrals, and AI governance. One infrastructure deployment. Four revenue streams. Every payer.
