Healthcare AI Infrastructure & Governance

Market Sizing: The Fastest-Growing Segment in Healthcare

The global AI in healthcare market was valued at $21.66 billion in 2025 and is projected to reach $110.61 billion by 2030 at a compound annual growth rate of 38.6%—the fastest growth rate of any healthcare technology segment. In the United States alone, the AI healthcare market was valued at $8.41 billion in 2024 and is projected to reach $195 billion by 2034 at a 36.94% CAGR. PwC Strategy& projects that AI's total impact on healthcare will reach $868 billion by 2030, comprising $646 billion in cost savings and $222 billion in revenue gains across the global healthcare system. North America alone is predicted to harness an AI potential of $479 billion by 2030.

Sources: MarketsandMarkets (2025), Precedence Research (2025), PwC Strategy& (2025)

The growth is not hypothetical. Clinical AI applications held the largest market share in 2024, with U.S. hospitals deploying AI in sepsis prediction, ICU deterioration alerts, radiology triage, stroke routing, diabetic retinopathy screening, and oncology treatment planning. Cloud-based AI deployment is the fastest-growing segment as health systems shift imaging workloads, ambient documentation engines, and predictive analytics to cloud environments. AI agents in healthcare specifically—autonomous systems that handle patient engagement, appointment scheduling, clinical decision support, and documentation—are growing at 44.1% CAGR, from $1.11 billion in 2025 to a projected $6.92 billion by 2030.

The Governance Gap: Existential, Urgent, and Unfilled

Healthcare AI is entering clinical workflows at unprecedented speed with virtually no governance infrastructure beneath it. The disconnect between AI capability and AI governance is not a future risk—it is a current crisis. The average healthcare data breach costs $7.42 million, the highest of any industry. Shadow AI usage—employees using AI tools without organizational approval or governance—is widespread and growing. Regulatory frameworks are fragmented: the International Telecommunication Union emphasizes six key governance areas (transparency, risk management, data quality, clinical validation, post-market surveillance, and ethical governance), while the International Medical Device Regulators Forum outlines 10 guiding principles for AI/ML-enabled medical devices. But the operational infrastructure to implement these frameworks in real clinical environments does not exist.

The PwC Strategy& finding is the most telling: only 15% of healthcare organizations feel fully prepared to implement AI business models. The remaining 85% lack comprehensive strategies, struggle with scalability, and cannot expand beyond internal efficiencies to commercial applications. The barrier is not the AI models themselves—every major technology company is building capable AI. The barrier is the governed, interoperable infrastructure that makes AI safe and useful in healthcare. AI models need to know what data they can access. They need to know what outputs are permissible. They need to be monitored for hallucination, drift, and adversarial manipulation in real time. None of this infrastructure exists as a standard layer today.
