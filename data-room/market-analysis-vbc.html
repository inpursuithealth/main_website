The Value-Based Care & Outcome-Based Payment Market

Every payer in the United States is transitioning from fee-for-service to outcome-based payment. 300,000 physicians need infrastructure to make the transition. CMS is the beachhead. Commercial payers are the scale. The global VBC services market reaches $4.45 trillion by 2030.

The Value-Based Care & Outcome-Based Payment Market

The Structural Shift: From Volume to Value

The transition from fee-for-service to value-based care is not a policy proposal. It is a structural reorganization of the largest sector of the American economy, and it is already underway. The global value-based healthcare services market reached $1.93 trillion in 2025 and is projected to grow to $4.45 trillion by 2030 at an 18.23% CAGR, driven by shifting reimbursement incentives that reward measurable patient outcomes, accelerated enrollment in managed Medicare options, and payer-provider alignment around integrated care delivery. In the United States alone, the value-based healthcare service market was valued at $4.01 trillion in 2024 and is expected to reach $6.16 trillion by 2030 at a 7.4% CAGR.

Sources: Mordor Intelligence (2025), Grand View Research (2025)

The growth trajectory is driven by converging pressures. Healthcare spending rebounded to its highest growth rate in more than two decades in 2023. CMS expects spending to continue outpacing national inflation for the next decade, with healthcare on track to reach more than 20% of GDP by 2033. Prices for hospital care covered by private insurance increased at more than twice the rate of stays covered by Medicare and Medicaid between 2014 and 2024. This cost trajectory is unsustainable for every payer category—federal, commercial, employer, and individual. The structural response is value-based care: pay for outcomes, not activities.

Source: CMS National Health Expenditure Projections (2025), Interwell Health (2025)

The patient population driving this transition is massive. According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, nearly 129 million Americans suffer from at least one chronic disease. Chronic conditions account for approximately 90% of the nation's $4.1 trillion in annual health care expenditures. These are the patients who consume the most resources, generate the highest costs, and benefit the most from coordinated, technology-enabled care management—exactly the patients that value-based payment models are designed to serve.

Source: CDC (2024)

The adoption trajectory is not linear—it is accelerating. By payment model, shared savings programs held a 31.51% revenue share of the VBC market in 2024, while capitation and global budget models are projected to post the fastest expansion at a 19.61% CAGR through 2030. By provider setting, hospitals and integrated delivery networks held 39.81% market share in 2024, while virtual and telehealth providers are on track for the fastest category growth at 24.55% CAGR. The message from the data is clear: VBC is expanding in every payment model category and every provider setting simultaneously.

CMS Payment Models: The Defined Revenue Accelerants

CMS is building the outcome-based ecosystem through multiple overlapping payment models, each with different mechanics but all requiring the same underlying infrastructure capabilities. The models are not competing with each other—they are complementary, and a single technology infrastructure partner can support a provider across all of them simultaneously. Understanding each model's structure is critical for sizing the addressable market.

The ACCESS Model: Technology-Enabled Chronic Care at Scale

The Advancing Chronic Care with Effective, Scalable Solutions (ACCESS) Model is a 10-year voluntary national model offered in all 50 states, U.S. territories, and the District of Columbia, operating from July 5, 2026 through June 30, 2036. It tests Outcome-Aligned Payments (OAPs)—a new payment option for Medicare-enrolled care organizations where participating organizations receive recurring payments for managing patients' qualifying chronic conditions, with full payment tied to achieving measurable health outcomes. Unlike traditional fee-for-service billing, which pays for defined activities, ACCESS pays for results—giving clinicians flexibility to deliver care however it is most effective, including through telehealth, remote monitoring, wearable devices, mobile applications, and AI-guided coaching.

Source: CMS ACCESS Model page, ACCESS RFA v1.1 (Feb 12, 2026)

Applications opened January 12, 2026, with the Cohort 1 deadline of April 1, 2026 (some sources note March 20, 2026 as an earlier portal deadline). Applications received after Cohort 1 will be considered for January 1, 2027 start. Rolling admissions continue through April 1, 2033, ensuring at least three years of participation for each participant. Over 250 organizations expressed interest at the December 2025 MAHA (Modernizing America's Health for All) event where the model was announced alongside the CMS Health Tech Ecosystem, which has grown to more than 450 participants preparing for initial ecosystem releases in March 2026.

Sources: CMS ACCESS Model page, CMS Innovation Center Blog (Dec 2025), Holland & Knight analysis (Dec 2025)

ACCESS addresses a longstanding payment gap in Original Medicare. Digital technologies—telehealth, wearable devices, health apps, and AI-powered care management—are transforming how care is delivered for chronic conditions, but Medicare's fee-for-service methodology pays for a defined set of activities that do not align with how technology-supported care works. Approximately two-thirds of Medicare beneficiaries live with chronic conditions that ACCESS is designed to address, including hypertension, diabetes, obesity, chronic kidney disease, cardiovascular disease, chronic pain, depression, and anxiety. The model explicitly targets populations in communities with limited local care options, especially rural areas.

ACCESS Clinical Tracks & Payment Structure

ACCESS organizes care into four clinical tracks, each grouping comorbid conditions that are commonly treated together. Each track includes condition-specific, guideline-informed outcome measures and targets developed in consultation with clinical experts. Payment is prospective: CMS pays 50% of OAPs monthly throughout each 12-month care period. The remaining 50% is withheld and reconciled at the end of each care period based on the Outcome Attainment Rate—the percentage of enrolled patients who meet their defined outcome targets.

The Outcome Attainment Threshold (OAT) for the first model year is 50%. This means an organization earns 100% of withheld payments if at least 50% of their aligned beneficiaries meet all required OAP Measure targets for their track during each care period. If fewer than 50% meet targets, payment is reduced proportionally, subject to a maximum reduction. The OAT increases in subsequent years, rewarding organizations that build sustained clinical capability. This structure is deliberately accessible—a 50% threshold in year one ensures that early participants are not penalized for the learning curve inherent in a new payment model.

Multi-track enrollment is permitted: patients can enroll in multiple clinical tracks simultaneously (e.g., a patient with hypertension and depression can be in both eCKM and BH). CMS applies a 5% discount to the lowest-cost overlapping track to account for shared care efficiencies. This is significant for revenue modeling: a patient population with high comorbidity prevalence generates higher per-patient revenue because each comorbid condition is a separate billable track.

Co-management payments to referring providers: CMS pays the patient's primary care physician or referring clinician approximately $30 per co-management service for documented review of care updates and care coordination (billable once every four months, up to ~$100/patient/year/track). An additional ~$10 one-time onboarding bonus applies per beneficiary. These payments are made directly by CMS to the referring provider—they are not payments from the ACCESS participant. The referring provider's existing fee-for-service billing is completely unaffected. No beneficiary cost-sharing applies to co-management payments. Participants may also elect to waive beneficiary cost-sharing on OAPs as a standardized beneficiary engagement incentive.

Source: ACCESS RFA v1.1 pp. 31-32, CMS Technical FAQ

Technology requirements are explicitly mandated. Participants must use FHIR-based APIs to report outcomes and electronically share clinical updates with referring clinicians. Services may include clinician consultations, lifestyle and behavioral support, medication management, care coordination, and use of FDA-cleared hardware and software devices. CMS will maintain a public directory listing each participant's tracks, tools, and risk-adjusted clinical outcomes. CMS will also host an ACCESS Tools Directory within the participant portal where vendors may list software and hardware tools (with optional promotional offers for ACCESS participants).

Why ACCESS is the primary revenue accelerant: It is the most defined, most funded, and most immediate CMS payment model for technology-enabled care. It has a 10-year duration, rolling admissions through 2033, explicit support for technology-first care delivery, and outcome-aligned payments that reward clinical results rather than activities. It targets the exact patient population (Medicare beneficiaries with chronic conditions) and provider population (small-to-mid-size practices needing technology partners) that the TETRA infrastructure is designed to serve. And CMS has explicitly stated that Medicare Advantage organizations may independently adopt similar payment arrangements, extending the revenue opportunity beyond Original Medicare.

The LEAD Model: The Longest ACO Test in CMS History

The Long-term Enhanced ACO Design (LEAD) Model is a 10-year model—the longest CMS Innovation Center has ever tested—beginning January 1, 2027 with applications accepted from March 2026. LEAD is designed specifically to bring smaller, independent, and rural practices into Accountable Care Organizations for the first time. It addresses the reality that most small practices have been unable to participate in ACO programs because the infrastructure and risk management capabilities required are beyond their resources.

LEAD offers two risk tracks: Global (100%/100% shared savings/losses) and Professional (50%/50%). It introduces CARA—CMS Administered Risk Arrangements—a new mechanism enabling digital data-sharing and payment between ACOs and preferred providers. LEAD also includes pilot ACO-Medicaid partnership frameworks in two states, signaling CMS's intent to extend the model beyond Medicare into Medicaid managed care. For infrastructure providers, LEAD represents a compound revenue opportunity: an organization already participating in ACCESS can layer LEAD ACO participation on top, using the same data infrastructure to serve both models simultaneously.

MSSP, TEAM & the Model Ecosystem

The Medicare Shared Savings Program (MSSP) is the largest ACO program in Medicare, with 511 ACOs, over 700,000 participating providers, and more than 13.2 million aligned beneficiaries. MSSP delivered $2.1 billion in net savings in 2023, proving that the accountable care model produces real cost reductions when the infrastructure supports it. Many MSSP ACOs are now transitioning from one-sided risk (upside only) to two-sided risk (upside and downside) under LEAD, creating urgent demand for more sophisticated data analytics, population health management, and risk stratification capabilities.

Source: CMS (2024)

The Transforming Episode Accountability Model (TEAM) is a mandatory episode-based payment model for surgical procedures across pre-operative, surgical, and post-acute care settings. TEAM requires seamless care coordination and data exchange across multiple providers—the exact interoperability challenge that fragmented EHR systems cannot solve without infrastructure. While TEAM is distinct from chronic care models like ACCESS and LEAD, it reinforces the same infrastructure dependency: providers need technology that connects data across care settings in real time.

The compound effect: ACCESS, LEAD, MSSP, and TEAM are not isolated programs. They are overlapping payment models that a single provider can participate in simultaneously. A small practice connected to infrastructure could have ACCESS patients in chronic care tracks, LEAD patients in an ACO arrangement, MSSP-attributed patients generating shared savings, and TEAM episodes requiring post-acute coordination—all flowing through the same data interoperability and orchestration layer. The infrastructure that supports one model supports all of them.

Commercial Payers: Following the CMS Blueprint

CMS models set the framework. Commercial payers adopt it. This is not speculation—it is the documented historical pattern across every major payment reform of the last two decades, and it is repeating now with increasing velocity. Vertically integrated entities now combine insurance, primary care, specialty services, pharmacy, and analytics within one operating entity. Optum manages 4.7 million patients under value-based contracts. Kaiser Permanente's Risant Health is adding Geisinger and Cone Health assets to expand its regional footprint. UnitedHealth Group's value-based payment volume increased more than 15% in a single year.

Sources: UnitedHealth Group Investor Presentation (2025), Mordor Intelligence (2025)

UnitedHealthcare / Optum

Largest commercial payer in the U.S. Optum manages 4.7 million patients under value-based contracts. Over 60% of provider payments are now tied to quality and value metrics. UHG has vertically integrated insurance, primary care (Optum Health), specialty pharmacy (Optum Rx), and analytics (Optum Insight) into a single operating entity. Their VBC payment volume has grown more than 15% annually. They are the proof point that value-based care at commercial scale works—and every provider participating in UHG arrangements needs the same data infrastructure.

Aetna / CVS Health

CVS Health's acquisition of Oak Street Health, integration with MinuteClinic, and Aetna payer operations positions them for Medicare VBC at enormous scale. The same interoperability challenge applies: thousands of independent provider practices generating data across incompatible systems, requiring infrastructure to connect, normalize, and make actionable. The pharmacy-payer-provider vertical integration strategy creates natural demand for an orchestration layer.

Medicare Advantage

Medicare Advantage enrollment reached 54% of eligible Medicare beneficiaries in 2024—35.7 million members—and continues to grow. CMS has explicitly stated that MA organizations may independently adopt outcome-aligned payment arrangements modeled on ACCESS. MA plans are watching ACCESS closely and building equivalent structures. TETRA infrastructure serves MA plans identically to Original Medicare. The MA market alone is larger than many entire industries.

Source: KFF (2024)

Medicaid & State Programs

Over 90 million Medicaid beneficiaries across 50 states. LEAD Model includes pilot ACO-Medicaid partnership frameworks in two states. Medicaid managed care organizations are building VBC arrangements. State Medicaid agencies are moving toward quality-based payments. The infrastructure requirement is identical: data interoperability, quality reporting, care coordination across fragmented community health systems and FQHCs.

The Addressable Provider Market

The primary target market is the small-to-mid-size physician practice with chronic care populations and no internal data infrastructure capability. Over 36% of U.S. physician practices have fewer than five physicians. These practices manage enormous patient panels—many with high chronic disease burden—but lack the data interoperability, population health analytics, risk stratification, clinical quality reporting, and cross-system care coordination capabilities required for participation in any value-based care arrangement under any payer.

Independent & Small Group Practices

~200,000

Primary care, internal medicine, family medicine, and specialty practices (endocrinology, cardiology, nephrology, behavioral health) managing chronic disease populations. Largest segment of the 300,000 physician market. These practices operate on EHR systems that cannot share data with the provider next door, let alone across payer boundaries. They cannot participate in ACCESS, LEAD, MSSP, or commercial VBC without a technology partner that provides the infrastructure at zero upfront cost.

VBC Provider Groups & IPAs

~3,500

Organized provider networks, Independent Practice Associations, and clinically integrated networks that already manage some downside risk. Many are transitioning from one-sided to two-sided risk under LEAD. They need more sophisticated population health analytics, real-time risk stratification, and cross-system interoperability to manage the additional financial exposure. Current infrastructure is often first-generation and inadequate for the complexity of new CMS models.

FQHCs & Rural Health Clinics

7,200+

1,500+ Federally Qualified Health Centers serving 52 million Americans at 17,000+ delivery sites. 5,700+ Rural Health Clinics in medically underserved areas. These organizations serve the highest chronic disease burden populations in the country and are explicitly targeted by ACCESS (with rural payment adjustments) and LEAD (with lower participation minimums). They represent the communities where technology-supported care can have the greatest impact.

The universal infrastructure requirement: Whether the payer is CMS, UnitedHealthcare, Aetna, a state Medicaid program, or a self-insured employer—the provider needs the same thing: data interoperability across their EHR, connected payer feeds, lab results, pharmacy data, and device data, made actionable at the point of care through governed AI, with quality metrics reporting and population health analytics. One infrastructure stack. Every payer. Every model. Every provider segment.
