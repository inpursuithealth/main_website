The Thesis

The United States spends $5.6 trillion annually on healthcare with outcomes that rank among the worst in the developed world. The root cause is structural: the fee-for-service model pays for volume, not value. Every major payer—CMS, UnitedHealthcare, Aetna, Cigna, Blue Cross, state Medicaid programs—is now actively building the replacement. CMS mandates 100% of Medicare beneficiaries in accountable care by 2030. The number of VBC-aligned patients is expected to double from 43 million (2022) to 90 million by 2027. Two-thirds of provider organizations have already increased VBC participation.

Sources: CMS Innovation Center, McKinsey, Mordor Intelligence, KFF

This transition cannot happen on existing infrastructure. Provider data is siloed across thousands of incompatible EHRs. Only 69% of U.S. inpatient settings have FHIR-based APIs. AI is entering clinical workflows with only 15% of organizations prepared. 129 million Americans have at least one chronic disease. The infrastructure gap between the mandate and reality is the opportunity.

The Four Converging Markets

I. Provider & Value-Based Care

$4.45T by 2030

300,000 physicians on fragmented EHR systems need infrastructure to transition to VBC. CMS ACCESS Model (July 2026, 10-year), LEAD (Jan 2027), MSSP (511 ACOs, $2.1B savings in 2023), TEAM. II. Direct-to-Consumer Health

330M+ Americans with smartphones. 35%+ already use health apps/wearables but data is fragmented across dozens of point solutions. No app unifies the full record. Four subscription tiers (Free/$14.99/$34.99/$49.99) + transaction-moment commerce ($12/user/yr). Medicare App Library (all 3 categories) + app stores + provider referral flywheel.

38.6% CAGR—fastest-growing healthcare segment. PwC projects $868B total AI impact by 2030. Every major AI company building toward healthcare; none have the governance layer. TETRA Conductor (model-agnostic orchestration), Aegis (patent-pending governance), Sentinel (behavioral surveillance). AI needs infrastructure beneath it.

IV. Interoperability & RPM

Compounds with VBC: ACCESS patients + non-ACCESS RPM/CCM patients at same practices through same infrastructure.

ACCESS Model: The Primary Revenue Accelerant

The ACCESS Model is a 10-year voluntary national model (July 2026–June 2036) testing Outcome-Aligned Payments for technology-enabled chronic care. CMS pays participating organizations fixed monthly payments per patient per clinical track, with full payment tied to measurable health outcomes. Four tracks target conditions affecting ~67% of Medicare beneficiaries: eCKM ($360/yr initial), CKM ($420/yr), MSK ($180/yr), BH ($180/yr). Patients can enroll in multiple tracks. CMS pays 50% monthly; remaining 50% reconciled on outcomes (50% attainment threshold in Year 1). Referring providers earn CMS co-management payments (~$100/patient/year/track) independently.

Source: ACCESS RFA v1.1 (Feb 12, 2026), CMS Technical FAQ, CMS Model Payment Amounts

Why ACCESS needs infrastructure: Participants must use FHIR-based APIs, electronically share clinical updates with referring clinicians, and demonstrate technology-enabled care. Small-to-mid-size practices (2,000 eligible patients average) cannot build this. They need a technology partner who provides the infrastructure, handles the billing complexity, and manages the outcomes reporting at zero upfront cost. Over 250 organizations expressed interest at the December 2025 MAHA announcement. MA plans may independently adopt similar arrangements.

The Infrastructure Moat: TETRA Stack

TETRA is not an app. It is payer-agnostic, model-agnostic, EHR-agnostic infrastructure. Six layers that other systems sit on top of:

TETRA™ Data Fabric

Foundation layer. Ingests, normalizes, and orchestrates health data across EHRs, payers, labs, pharmacies, and devices.

TETRA eX™ Exchange

Bidirectional TEFCA/QHIN-compliant data exchange. Connects to national health information networks.

TETRA Conductor™ AI Orchestration

Model-agnostic. Routes each clinical interaction to the optimal AI model with appropriate data access and governance.

TETRA Vault™ Consumer Data

Zero-knowledge consumer health data container. Patient owns and controls their unified record.

TETRA Aegis™ AI Governance

Autonomous supervisory control plane. Patent-pending (Feb 2026). Governs what data AI can access and what outputs are permissible.

TETRA Sentinel™ Protectionary AI

Continuous behavioral surveillance of AI systems. Hallucination detection, drift monitoring, adversarial defense. Independent of supervised models.

Market Dynamics: Accelerants & Risk Factors

Accelerants

CMS regulatory momentum: ACCESS (Jul 2026), LEAD (Jan 2027), expanding MSSP, TEFCA mandates create compounding urgency.

Commercial payer adoption: UHC, Aetna, BCBS building VBC modeled on CMS. Same infrastructure, no modification.

AI governance demand: 85% of organizations unprepared. Governance infrastructure demand grows faster than AI market itself.

Info-blocking enforcement: $1M/violation penalties create board-level urgency.

Medicare App Library: Government-backed distribution at scale no marketing budget replicates.

Risk Factors

Provider adoption pace: Small practices risk-averse. Conservative 1 → 10 → 50 ramp reflects this. Education and demonstrated results required.

CMS model execution: ACCESS is new. Rates and thresholds may adjust. 10-year duration and rolling admissions mitigate.

Regulatory changes: Administration shifts could alter CMMI priorities. OBBBA (Jul 2025) provides legislative anchoring.

Competitive entry: Large EHR vendors could build competing layers. None currently combine all six. Federal validation creates proof-point moat.

Reimbursement risk: OAP rates or RPM codes could change. Four revenue streams diversify dependence.

Revenue projections are detailed in the 3-Year Pro Forma, distributed separately. Conservative model: 1 → 10 → 50 providers, 2,000 eligible patients per practice, 10K → 75K → 250K D2C users. The market opportunity dwarfs the conservative assumptions by orders of magnitude.
